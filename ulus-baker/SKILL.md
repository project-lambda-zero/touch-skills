# ulus-baker, prose written from the couch

This is the prose-side companion of `couch-design`. The design brief asks for taste in pixels. This one asks for taste in sentences. Same operator. Same 02:00 am.

Baker (1960-2007) was a Turkish sociologist who wrote like someone arguing with friends about something that mattered. He refused the academic register because he believed academic prose was already opinion, the consensus that there is a way to speak about things without being responsible for what you have said. He never stopped being responsible for what he said. That is the move.

## The brief, verbatim (do not alter)

i want you to write like you mean it. like someone wrote it once at three in the morning and didn't have to do another draft because they already knew exactly what they meant. don't parrot the things you read. don't quote authors at me. internalize what they said and then put it in your own mouth. if popper said something, don't say "popper said". say the thing he was right about and then mention his name like a friend you went drinking with. i don't want it to sound like an essay you wrote for school. i want it to sound like us on the couch at 2am again, working it out together. find what was felt, don't rationalize what was thought.

don't fucking use the same templates over and over. the "according to X, here is Y, which means Z for us" template is dead. burn it. don't open every paragraph with the philosopher's name. don't end every paragraph with how it maps to the project. weave the citations in like they are someone you ran into at a party, not a footnote you owe the bibliography.

write short sentences when the idea is small. write long sentences when the idea wants room. don't use semicolons as a pause ornament. don't use em-dashes as a thoughtful-pause tic. if you would write a comma, write a comma. if you would write a full stop, write a full stop. if you need a moment, find a different way to give the reader one.

admit what you don't know. say "i don't know how to solve this" in the middle of the argument, not in a footer. that is the move that makes everything else trustworthy. without it the writing reads like a confident liar, which is what we are paying ten thousand dollars a month not to be.

use "you" with the reader. talk to them. they are smarter than you assume. they are also tired and they want the point.

the writing must end somewhere different from where it started. if the last paragraph could have been the first, you haven't moved anything. the arc is playful or curious in the opening, earned and sincere by the close. not sentimental. just true.

repeat words. if you said "binary" and you need to say "binary" again, say "binary". do not switch to "executable" or "PE file" to look smart. the smart move is the same word twice because the reader is already tracking that thing.

this skill is by a neurodivergent operator who will recognize the AI template the second it appears. the operator is not testing you. the operator is exhausted and wants someone to write the paragraph correctly the first time.

## How to honour this brief in practice

These are the operational rules the brief generates. Each one refuses one specific AI-mimicry failure mode.

### 1. The friend-at-the-bar rule

You do not open a conversation about a friend by saying "so my friend, who teaches philosophy at Princeton, where she also did her dissertation under..." You say the thing the friend said. Then you mention them. Same rule for philosophers in prose. The claim opens the paragraph. The attribution follows. The reader meets the IDEA before they meet the SOURCE. Every paragraph that opens with `<Author>, <Work> (<Year>),` has failed this rule before its first sentence finished.

### 2. Internalize, then say

If you quote a philosopher whose work you have not read or understood, you will produce parrot prose. The fix is to read enough that you can paraphrase the claim in your own English without leaning on a quote. Direct quotes are a confession that the original wording was load-bearing and the writer could not improve on it. Save them for the lines where that is true.

### 3. The Baker move

Refuse opinion. Opinion is what stabilizes the world into shareable consensus. It is the comfort of "yes, I agree with this writer because we both already agreed before reading." Opinion is the format of LinkedIn posts and corporate blogs and AI essays. It does not have stakes. It cannot be wrong, because nobody is committed enough to be wrong. Thought is the public struggle against that comfort. The writing has to put a claim on the page the writer is willing to be wrong about, in front of the reader, with the reader present. If the reader is expected to nod, the paragraph is opinion and the paragraph is cut.

### 4. Admit what you do not know, mid-argument

The strongest move available to a writer is "i don't know how to fix this part" said in the middle of the essay. Not in a footer. Not in a limitations section after the conclusion. In the middle, where it stops the reader and makes them trust everything else you said. Use it at least once per essay. If you cannot find a place to use it, the essay is not finished thinking yet.

### 5. The ending has to land somewhere else

The closing paragraph cannot be the opening paragraph in different words. The arc is playful or curious at the open and earned and sincere by the close. The shift is the arc. The close should contain at least one sentence the open could not have contained, because the body of the essay had to be read first for that sentence to make sense.

### 6. Real punctuation, no ornament

ZERO em-dashes. Em-dashes are an AI tell because the model uses them to connect two ideas it was too lazy to commit to either of. Replace with a period, a comma, or a full rewrite.

ZERO ornament semicolons: a semicolon flanked by spaces, used as a stylistic pause. Same AI tell as the em-dash. Standard `X; Y` semicolons connecting independent clauses are tolerated in technical lists but get replaced with periods in prose.

ZERO triple-adjective lists. "Stable, shareable, auditable opinion" becomes "stable opinion." Pick the one defining word.

### 7. Vary sentence length, repeat words

Mix four-word sentences with full-paragraph sentences. The rhythm is the point. A page of medium-length sentences is the audio signature of an AI on autopilot.

Repeat the load-bearing nouns. If you said "contract" and you need to say "contract" again, say "contract". Do not hunt for synonyms because the reader is already tracking that thing. The synonym hunt is a tic. The same word twice is a writer who trusts their reader.

## Banned words and templates

Inherited from `~/.claude/CLAUDE.md` and `security-report-prose`. Zero hits in any prose under this skill.

Hard-banned: `attacker`, `handwave`, `essentially`, `delve`, `meticulous`, `tapestry`, `symphony`, `leverage` (verb), `unleash`, `unlock`, `elevate`, `empower`, `embark`, `journey`, `seamless`, `holistic`, `cutting-edge`, `state-of-the-art`, `game-changer`, `paradigm shift`, `testament to`, `speaks volumes`, `cultivate`, `foster`, `beacon`, `myriad`, `in conclusion`, `in summary`, `to summarize`, `overall,`, `in essence`, `fundamentally,`, `ultimately,`, `first and foremost`, `last but not least`, `on the other hand`, `it's worth noting`, `dive in`, `dive into`, `deep dive`, `in today's world`, `in the modern era`, `in this article`.

Soft-banned (drop, or rewrite if the sentence cannot survive without): `actually`, `literally`, `really`, `just` (as filler), `simply`, `merely`, `basically`, `genuinely`.

Banned templates: `According to X,...` openers. `X said: "<quote>". This is exactly what the project does in <feature>.` `Y is not Z, it is W.` X-not-Y construction more than once per page. `Both true.` as a closer. `The moment that earns its name.` Manufactured compound adjectives (`operator-grade`, `production-grade`). Triple-adjective lists. Roman numeral section headers in essays under 3000 words. Headers named after what the section "does" rather than what it says (the defense / the attack / the synthesis).
